CS Course Advice

Overview

Every quarter, you are assigned two pass times - Pass 1 and Pass 2. Unfortunately, picking classes is often a troubling and difficult task as we college students suffer from decision fatigue, incomplete ratemyprofessor listings, course conflicts, and the neverending waitlist. This page serves to help you make superior decisions and better utilize your time and schedule with your long-term agenda. For each class, there is a subject matter breakdown, relevant fields in Computer Science, importance in regard to employability, professor recommendations, and general advice.

Choosing Courses

The purpose of college, for most people, is to obtain a degree and learn knowledge sufficient to succeed in the workforce. As such, it is in your interests to take as many major-related courses as possible. As such, when choosing between a CS class and non-cs class, the typical superior choice will always be to take the CS classes. Please note that there exist exceptions (rare classes, retiring professors, etc). Next, when choosing between two CS classes, choose in this order.

  1. If you have rare circumstances (retiring professor, rare offerings, etc.), take it.
  2. If you are interested in one class over another, take it.
  3. If one is more useful for the workforce, take it.
  4. If one opens up more potentially available classes, take it. (e.g. ECS 154A and 140A are both required. One might also argue that they are both useful, though I beg to differ. ECS 140A opens up 140B, 142, 160 and 170 while ECS 154A opens up 154B. Therefore, ECS 140A is the superior option.)
  5. If you are choosing between professors for the same class, take the one with better reviews - talk to your peers and their past students to try and get a gauge of your future performance in the class. Also, try to find their course sites and look at lectures/materials. These will often be the best measure for estimating success in a class.
Finally, for choosing professors, try to go for the more difficult professor, who is often better at teaching. The purpose of classes is to learn, not breeze through college and be useless afterward. Use GE's to boost your GPA, not your major-related classes (unless you don't care about the required class. e.g. ECS 154A for me).

Pass Times & Registration

It is incredibly important to be cognizant of your pass time dates and times. Missing these times has severe consequences, as you lose a great edge over some of your peers in getting classes you need. Often times, missing your date/time means being put a quarter back. I also highly recommend planning out your long-term schedule far ahead of time and checking it over with advisors. Then, around one-two days before your pass time, update your schedule to align with more detailed logistics such as specific times, professors, and classmates. Waiting until your pass time is imminent is a better use of time, since if you plan too early, you will have to drastically change your plan due to desired classes filling up.

One trick you can use to get around competing with your peers is to make friends with upperclassmen. If they have not yet hit the 17 unit cap, they can sign up for the classes you want, then you can later coordinate with them to get their spot in the class later on. Be sure to take them out for dinner or otherwise pay them back; few people are willing to go to so much trouble for others.

Waitlisting

Waitlisting is only allowed during Pass 2. When you are deciding to waitlist, I recommend waitlisting if you have no loss. However, if you have to drop a class in order to waitlist, you need to be almost entirely certain that you will be able to take the class. This can be done through analyzing past drop patterns. Typically, large classes will allow the first 30 or so students off the waitlist. As class sizes get smaller, this number often drops down to less than 10. There are exceptions - for example, ECS 60 has a 33%-50% drop rate. When posting on forums such as the CS club page, ask about drop rates - not about whether or not you can get off the waitlist. Nobody knows. Lastly, try to use all the features of ScheduleBuilder/Sisweb. They allow you to swap a class for another, where you will only drop one class if you successfully enroll in the other. This works much better than dropping a class, then scrambling to pick up the next.

Sometimes, there are rare circumstances. If you want to take two classes that occur at the same time, get a PTA from the more lenient professor. If you use the PTA to waitlist, the system will hold you back from getting the class, due to the conflict even though you already previously entered a PTA. For this situation, you can either ask the other (non-waitlisted) course's professor for a PTA, or inform Natasha. She can bypass the system and grant you a PTA for the non-waitlisted course. This way, you don't have to convince the non-waitlisted course's professor as well.

Permission To Add (PTA)

Permission to Add numbers are the only things that can really bypass ScheduleBuilder/Sisweb. Use these sparingly. They can be obtained through messaging and convincing the professor directly, from convincing Natasha, or from the College of Letters and Sciences. These will also allow you to late-add a class, though it takes much more effort to convince administrators in these circumstances. When you try to convince them, don't talk about your own needs and wants, as those are insufficient grounds to obtain a PTA number. Rather, complain about aspects out of your control - family health, emergencies, personal health, etc.

Permission To Drop (PTD)

Permission to Drop numbers are surprisingly similar to PTA numbers in that they are rather difficult to obtain. First, it is important to be aware of drop deadlines - some classes have 10 day drops while others have 20. Luckily, most CS courses have 20 day drops. After this deadline, you can ask your professors for PTD numbers due to personal issues, or basically the reasons possible for obtaining PTA numbers. Always ask the professor first, and keep in mind they have no obligation to help you. If this doesn't work, try convincing people at the College of Letters and Sciences advising. I recommend not talking to the main older lady, who works in a cubicle next to reception. She is exceedingly difficult to get past. Meanwhile, there is a younger woman there who is much nicer. Try to get an appointment with her. Be sure to bring supporting documents, since scheduling appointments takes a long time.

Choosing General Education (GE)s

CS Majors are in the College of Letters & Sciences. As such, we are required to take GE's to fulfill a certain set of parameters. The two main sections are Topical Breadth and Core Literacies. No class can be used to fulfill two requirements in the same section. However, one class can be used once in each. Following the idea that it is in your best interest to take more CS classes, you want to minimize the number of GE's needed. Try to pick classes that can be used once in each section, and not to overlap requirements (such that one becomes useless). While you take GE's as well as after, it is also better to take classes you're interested in learning about - that is the original purpose of taking GE's, that you can expand your breadth knowledge as well.

Course Preparation

Although most people don't typically prepare for courses, doing so may prove to be incredibly helpful. Reading the textbook ahead of time or talking with past students about their projects (and how they work) is often helpful - verifying your knowledge is much easier than gaining it in the first place. The only downside is this takes personal motivation and time.

Choosing a Major Track

Throughout your life, people often ask "What do you want to do"? Now's (unfortunately), the time to decide. I believe it's important to first make the choice between research and the workforce. In order to make this choice, you should basically try research for reasons detailed in the Research section. Next, I believe it's important to understand what each major track feeds into later on. The main tracks offered by the department are (a) Databases and Data Management, (b) Graphics and Visualization, (c) Networking, (d) Security and Cryptography, and (e) Systems and Software Engineering.

If you're interested in databases, big data, and backend, I recommend taking STA classes and ECS 165AB. Unfortunately, Davis doesn't teach NoSQL databases, so try and learn those on your own time, online. Be sure to have a strong understanding of relational algebra.

If you're interested in Graphics and Visualization, then take ECS 163, 175, 177, and 178. Also, ask Amenta about her work/research, and if you can potentially join as well. There is a visualization lab in Kemper you probably want to aquaint yourself with.

If you're interested in Networking, take ECS 152ABC.

If you're interested in Security and Cryptography, take ECS 153, talk to Professor Rogaway, Chen, and Bishop for potential research. It is important to note that ECS 150 (Operating Systems) and ECS 152A (Networks) are incredibly difficult for Security.

Finally, you have general software development. I recommend focusing on and rushing for ECS 192 (Senior Design), ECS 140A, and ECS 160. These will teach you programming languages, popular design patterns, have you work on a project for a company in a group (extremely helpful), and Mobile App Development.

Technology Management Minor

There is an increasingly popular minor called Technology Management. This is a unique minor in that it gives you connections at the Graduate School of Management, makes you apply to declare, and is helpful if you are hoping to work with Product/Project Management later on.

Priority Registration (UHP & Athletics)

As you might quickly notice, it is often impossibly difficult to get the classes you need/are interested in. If you are a good student, there is a way to get around this. Students in the honors program get priority registration, and this list of students is refreshed every year. If you have above a 3.5, you can apply to enter into the Honors Program. Although you won't be able to get the monetary scholarship included in the program if you are chosen as an incoming freshman, you still get priority registration and the opportunity to take honors classes. Additionally, since this list is refreshed every year, you can keep priority registration for the year even if your GPA drops below 3.5. Another way to get priority registration is to become a student athlete. Finally, the last way is to become an RA.
If you are a low-income freshman, you also have the opportunity to apply for STEP, which gives priority registration for Winter, Spring, then Fall of your first and second years. This program also lets you live in the dorms for a month prior to school to help you adjust.
Priority Registration is an amazing luxury everyone should strive for - it lets you take any class you want (e.g. PE classes that typically fill up after a week), opportunities to network and learn from closer relationships with professors, and potentially work on an Undergraduate Thesis and do research.

ECS 10: Introduction to Programming

Summary:

The purpose of ECS 10 is to teach you basic programming concepts of Computer Science using Python. It concentrates on problem solving through programming. Most CS/CSE majors either skip this class if they have prior programming experience or take it their first quarter at Davis. I recommend against taking this class to learn Python, as you can easily learn the same/more material online in less time, if you have experience in other languages. Also, you are not allowed to take this class for units if you have taken a 'higher' class (e.g. ECS 30, 40, etc).

This class overlaps with ECS 12, ECS 15, ECS 30, and ENG 6. It does not cover memory management, pointers, or arrays which are covered in ECS 30.

Prerequisites

Two years of high school algebra.

Topics Covered

  • Primitive Data Types
  • Assignment Statements
  • Input & Output Statements
  • Functions Declarations and Calls
  • While & For Loops
  • Variable Scope
  • Data Structures:
    • Lists
    • Strings
    • Dictionaries
    • Objects
  • Debugging

Workload & Time Commitment

Asking Friends

When to Take

Take this class as soon as possible, preferably during your first quarter. If you can, try to skip this class altogether. Learning Python is relatively simple if you can do well in ECS 30. Also, you will be at a level such that you will succeed in ECS 30 if you have strong logical reasoning and a month or so of practice programming on your own. Personally, I recommend EDX's Introduction to Programming class online. The professors are at MIT, and the online class goes over ECS 30, 40, and parts of 60 - all in Python.

Usefulness for Research or Internships

The course material from this class is absolutely necessary knowledge for research or internships. Without understanding even simple variables and functions, one would be essentially useless in the workforce. Be sure to have a commanding understanding of this material for interviews.

ECS 12: Introduction to Media Computation

Summary

This class is a joint class that teaches Processing, which is a framework built on top of Java. It is typically targeted towards CTS students (equivalent to CTS 12), but under the CS department. This class focuses on using Processing, which is a tool created for artists to create simple programs based around graphical output. It abstracts away some of the technical details (listeners, etc) to facilitate ease of use. Additionally, students learn about how media is stored by computers, and how to manipulate it programmatically.

Prerequisites

None.

Topics Covered

Programming Aspects:

  • Variables and Objects
  • Conditionals and Loops
  • Functions
  • Arrays
  • Simple Data Structures
  • Drawing and moving basic shapes: Lines, Curves, Polygons

Representing Media

  • Signals
  • Analog vs. Digital
  • Sampling, filtering, discretization, sampling theorem
  • Vector vs. Rastor Graphics
  • Sound, images, movies
  • Storage and Transmission
  • Compression, Formats, Codecs

Computational and Programmatical Problem Solving

  • Analyzing and solving problems logically and analytically
  • Trends

Interaction

  • Input Hardware
  • Programming for Interaction
  • Event Processing & Callback Functions

Time-Based Media

  • Sounds
  • Animation

Extra Topics

  • Games
  • Digital Forgery
  • Fonts

Workload & Time Commitment

Similar to ECS 10 and 30, this class may take a great amount of time or very little depending on your programming background and critical thinking. Some people are able to understand concepts for this class extremely quickly while others are not. Friends of mine who took this course with no prior experience typically spent 6 hours per assignment.

When to Take

As a Computer Science major, you should never take this class. You only get two units if you have completed ECS 10 or 30, which are actually recommended and required for the major.

Usefulness for Research or Internships

The experience in Java and Graphics is often nice, as well as the knowledge of basic programming constructs. However, I know of very few job opportunities exclusively for Processing, so this course is not really useful. The programming constructs are, but there are better courses to take to learn these. The knowledge from this class will likely be helpful for further Visualization and Graphics.

ECS 30: Programming and Problem Solving

Summary:

The purpose of ECS 30 is to expand on basic programming concepts into lower level sophistication using C. This includes memory management, further abstract thinking, and debugging. This is the class that most CS/CSE majors take during their first quarter at Davis; it is imperative to get past this requirement as soon as possible because completing this requirement opens up many other future courses. I encourage thoroughly learning about pointers during this time to ensure success in ECS 40 and 60. Additionally, Linux is popularly used for this class, as are debuggers. Although these present a steep learning curve, gaining confidence and ease in using these tools is incredibly helpful for future classes.

Prerequisites

MAT 16A or 21A, experience with basic programming concepts (variables, loops, conditional statements).

Topics Covered

  • UNIX tools (vi, emacs, make, gdb)
  • C Programming Language
  • Data Types, Operators, Flow control
  • Top-down Design
  • Functions Declarations and Calls
  • Simple Data Structures
  • Pointers
  • Program Input Output
  • Modular Programming
  • Dynamic Memory management; Run-time storage allocation
  • Preprocessor Directives
  • Recursion

Workload & Time Commitment

Depending on your confidence with programming from previous experience, people have drastically varying experiences with this course.

When to Take

Take this class as early as possible - in your first or second quarter. It is essential for jobs and reaching further Computer Science classes.

Usefulness for Research or Internships

This class is absolutely required for getting a job or research. Without the simple, basic concepts from this course it will be incredibly difficult, nigh impossible, to succeed in future classes or jobs. Furthermore, this class does not provide sufficient knowledge for internships. Typically, it is important to reach at least ECS 60 (Data Structures) to do well in interviews.

ECS 60: Data Structures and Programming

Summary

ECS 60 is probably the most important lower division class you can take. It focuses on data structures, which is absolutely essential for any Computer Science job interview. I highly recommend taking this class as early as possible, preferably during your first year so you can use the knowledge for interviews. If you can, try taking the class with Sean. Although you will likely spend a large amount of time on the assignments, the level of competence you will reach is invaluable for the rest of your career.

Prerequisites

ECS 40, ECS 20, mastery of C++, knowledge of Big O.

Topics Covered

This course changes slightly each time, but these are common topics:

  • Stacks
  • Queues
  • CursorList
  • SkipList
  • Binary Heap
  • AVL Trees
  • Splay Trees
  • Hashes - Separate Chaining, Double Hashing, Quadratic Hashing, Pointer Hashes
  • Djikstras
  • Prim
  • Kruskal
  • Ford Fulkerson
  • BFS and DFS
  • B+ Trees

Sean does not cover red black trees, though some other professors might.

Workload & Time Commitment

People have widely varying time commitments for this class depending on interest and study habits. Personally, I've found that if you seek to really understand why a data structure is the way it is - its strengths and weaknesses, use cases, and design, then studying will be extremely easy and straightforward for you. However, if you try and brute-force memorize the algorithms, you will shortly forget the information and might not do well on the test. I know some people who have taken weeks on the second program, which is usually B+ Tree insert or delete, and others for whom it took around 5 hours. Seek to understand the program and concepts before starting.

When to Take

Take this class as early as possible, as it is crucial for internships and useful for projects overall.

Usefulness for Research or Internships

This class is absolutely crucial, and is typically the minimum required for research.

ECS 89A-L: Special Topics in Computer Science

Summary

Classes in this may vary, but the most popular/frequently offered 89 course is likely Computer Graphics, or ECS 89H taught by Nina Amenta. The class should really be called Web Development, as it covers client/server side programming, Node, JS, HTML, and CSS. This is useful for many web development internships and opportunities.

Prerequisites

ECS 30, UNIX knowledge

Topics Covered

This class focuses on front-end development with HTML/CSS/D3.js/JavaScript through the DOM and back-end development using Node.js

  • HTML
  • CSS
  • D3.js
  • Node
  • DOM
  • MVC Design Pattern
  • SQL
  • Routing
  • Asynchronous Programming
  • APIs
  • AJAX
  • SQL Injection
  • Cross site Scripting

Workload & Time Commitment

This class typically takes around 3-4 hours per assignment for weekly assignments. The first few can be completed in a mere several minutes. Tests are extremely straightforward and there is usually a large curve.

When to Take

Although this class doesn't actually fulfill any requirements, the knowledge covered is extremely helpful for job applications and job openings, as it gives a clear understanding of how client/server side programming works. On top of that, it is a good grade boost.

Usefulness for Research or Internships

This is extremely helpful for any web related internship. API's are also essential knowledge, and this is one of the few classes that covers APi's.
